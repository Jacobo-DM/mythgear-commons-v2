# SpiderVine
**Body-powered line travel & movement gear for the commons.**

Concept by Jacobo Hernández Durbin. Braided with Elumman + the Braid.

SpiderVine is a human-powered movement system for traveling along a suspended line or cable using the whole body — hands, core, and legs — rather than motors or passive glide.

It sits at the intersection of:
- movement medicine
- repairable mechanical design
- play, learning, and real-world utility

SpiderVine belongs to **MythGear** because it is not a vehicle in the consumer sense — it is *gear for inhabiting gravity*.

---

## Two modes

**Spider / Vine mode — one cable**
Less stable by design. More demanding, more skill-intensive. Best suited for challenge, training, play, and high-attention movement where instability is part of the point.

**Line mode — two cables**
More stable by comparison. For steadier movement and practical use: carrying tools or gear, doing temporary work, moving with more control, or operating where extra stability is needed.

The body does the work.  
The line carries the path.

---

## What it's for

- **Movement & conditioning** — full-body engagement, coordination, and proprioception.
- **Learning through difficulty** — a steep learning curve by design. Skill develops through attention, not automation.
- **Utility & resilience** — when equipped with a winch or pulley, SpiderVine can assist with lifting, hauling, temporary access, or emergency scenarios.
- **Play (serious play)** — fun is not an accident here. It's how learning sticks.

---

## What it is not

- Not a zipline
- Not a motorized system
- Not a turnkey product
- Not optimized for speed or comfort first

SpiderVine prioritizes **control, feedback, and repairability** over convenience.

---

## Safety note

SpiderVine is a concept and prototype direction. This README does not describe a tested or certified safety device. Any physical implementation requires proper engineering review, safety assessment, and appropriate training before use.

---

## Design questions in progress

- Single cable vs dual cable tradeoffs
- Hand-cycle vs mixed hand/leg drive
- Brake ergonomics and safety margins
- Learning curve design (how steep is *useful*?)
- Modular attachments (winch, tool pickup, rescue)

These questions are features, not problems.
