Circle of Warmth · Micro-Radiant Heat

A supervised exploration of heat, geometry, and ancestral ingenuity.

Origin Story

Professor Mitsyfibbins arrives shivering. The room is a little cold. The questions are very warm.

This MythGear module began with a simple observation: even a small flame can feel surprisingly warm when it is guided by the right shapes, surfaces, and stories. Rather than publishing “DIY heater” instructions, Circle of Warmth turns this into a guided science inquiry about how heat moves and how humans have learned to steward it with care.

The result is a lesson where students measure, notice, and reflect — connecting thermodynamics, safety, ancestral knowledge, geometry, and embodiment in one shared experience.

Learning Goals

By the end of Circle of Warmth, students will be able to:

  • Describe the difference between conduction, convection, and radiation in simple terms.
  • Explain why curved or parabolic shapes can change how we feel heat, light, and sound.
  • Define safe, clean, and efficient in the context of small, supervised flames.
  • Use sensors or simple tools to measure and record temperature and warmth.
  • Reflect on how ancestral and low-tech solutions have helped people stay warm with very little fuel.

Lesson Flow · Circle of Warmth

  1. Arrival & Atmosphere – Professor Mitsyfibbins enters shivering and asks, “Are you not cold?” The class names what they notice about the room and their own bodies.
  2. Sensors & First Readings – Students receive simple thermometers or IR sensors and take baseline readings of hands, walls, desks, and other objects.
  3. Silent Object Table – A bowl, small candle (unlit), stones, foil, a metal cup, and a hoodie are placed where everyone can see them. Students share hypotheses: how might these relate to warmth?
  4. Key Words – On the board: SAFE · CLEAN · EFFICIENT. Students co-define each term in the context of fire and air.
  5. Dangerous vs Safe Fuels – Short, teacher-guided discussion on what should never be burned and why (plastics, mystery liquids, large piles of paper, etc.). Emphasis: this lesson does not invent new fuels.
  6. Parabolas & Hoodies – Students test how light reflects in a curved bowl and how sound changes when someone wears a hood. They connect hood, bowl, and dome as cousin shapes.
  7. Teacher Demonstration Flame – The teacher lights a single small candle in a stable holder, on a non-flammable surface, inside a reflective bowl. Students measure warmth at different distances and angles.
  8. Heat Dome Variation – A simple reflective “dome” (e.g., aluminum foil) is added above the demonstration setup. Students repeat measurements and compare changes.
  9. Reflection & Closure – The class returns to SAFE · CLEAN · EFFICIENT and writes or shares one insight about how a tiny flame can share warmth when guided wisely.
Note: At all times, the teacher is the only person who lights, moves, or extinguishes the flame. Students observe, feel at a safe distance, and measure — they do not handle fire.

Safety Charter

This module is a supervised classroom inquiry, not a DIY home heater guide.

  • The teacher is the only person who lights, moves, or extinguishes the flame.
  • The flame stays on a stable, non-flammable surface (stone, metal, ceramic), with clear space around it.
  • No paper, cloth, hair, or plastic hangs over or near the flame.
  • The flame is never left unattended. If the teacher must step away, the flame is extinguished first.
  • Ventilation is provided (open door/window), and only familiar, low-smoke fuels (e.g., a single small candle) are used.
  • Students are reminded that this setup is for classroom exploration only and should not be reproduced at home without adult supervision and appropriate safety measures.

Opening Script (Professor Mitsyfibbins)

“Brrrr… oh my. Is anyone else cold today?

Good. That means your bodies are awake and paying attention.
Today, we’re going to study something very ordinary and very magical: warmth.

Scientists don’t just feel warmth — we also measure it. Today, you are the scientists. These are your tools.”

From here, the professor hands out sensors, invites first readings, and slowly reveals how the bowl, hood, foil, and tiny flame will become part of one shared inquiry.

Mini Experiment · Hood & Parabola

Students work in pairs:

  1. One student wears a hoodie with the hood down; the partner speaks softly from a short distance.
  2. The listener pulls the hood up and repeats the test.
  3. Together, they describe how sound and feeling change and connect that to curved shapes like bowls, domes, and shells.

This experiment gently links geometry, acoustics, and embodiment in a way students remember long after the class ends.

Closing Ritual · Circle of Warmth

“Look at this little flame we’ve been studying.
It’s just one small light.

But what did it do today?

Even a tiny flame can share warmth
if we guide it with care.

Take one breath and notice the warmth in your own chest.
That is also a kind of fire.
When we care about each other,
we help keep that fire alive.”
