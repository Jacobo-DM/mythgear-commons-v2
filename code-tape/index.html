CodeTape

A commons system for sharing useful local information without surveillance or extraction: through marks, strips, tags, prompts, and low-power signaling rooted in stewardship.

What it is

CodeTape is a commons method for marking, sensing, and sharing information that matters to a community without handing that information over to extractive platforms.

It may appear as a visible mark, an adhesive strip, a tagged object, a field prompt, or a low-power communication path embedded in roads, gardens, homes, trails, shorelines, boats, tools, and shared infrastructure.

Sometimes it is only a sign of permission and reciprocity. Sometimes it is a simple sensing strip with low-power communication. In either case, the point is the same: help people remember, warn, coordinate, and care for a place together.

Core idea: CodeTape is for local usefulness, not remote extraction.

How it appears

As mark

A visible sign that something is meant to be shared, repaired, adapted, or stewarded in common.

As strip

A segmentable tape-like substrate carrying simple sensors, identifiers, or signals that can be placed in the world.

As tag

A local-readable point of contact for instructions, warnings, histories, or maintenance knowledge.

As protocol

A way of deciding what gets shared, with whom, and under what community norms.

What it’s for

  • Sharing practical local information without surveillance or platform capture
  • Marking things for repair, adaptation, and commons reuse
  • Signaling conditions that matter to a neighborhood, crew, class, or steward circle
  • Supporting memory, warning, coordination, and distributed care
  • Building low-cost infrastructure that remains legible to ordinary people

Possible forms

A CodeTape implementation can be extremely simple or moderately technical.

At the lightest end, it may just be a mark and a shared meaning. At the heavier end, it may include simple sensors and low-power communication using NFC, Bluetooth LE, optical signaling, or local mesh links.

The important thing is not complexity. The important thing is that the information remains locally useful, transparent in intent, and difficult to appropriate for extraction.

mark strip tag nfc ble optical mesh

Where it can be used

  • Road edges, curves, crossings, and flood-prone routes
  • Gardens, trails, schoolyards, and field sites
  • Homes, fridges, medicine cabinets, and shared workshops
  • Marine systems, buoys, docks, and small vessels
  • Community infrastructure that benefits from local warning or memory
Not every use needs electronics. Sometimes a CodeTape object only needs a mark, a pattern, and a trusted local reading practice.

Design principles

  • Low cost and open enough to be copied, repaired, and taught
  • Biodegradable or recyclable materials where possible
  • Local encryption and local control where signaling is used
  • Adaptable to local climates, materials, and cultural contexts
  • Governed by stewardship circles rather than proprietary control

What it’s not

  • Not a surveillance platform
  • Not a product ecosystem built around lock-in
  • Not “smart” infrastructure for data extraction
  • Not a substitute for local judgment, care, or governance

CodeTape should remain transparent, non-exploitative, and answerable to the people living with it.

Status

CodeTape is still a blueprint more than a finished system. The next good step is to publish simple, concrete pieces: a mark, one physical pattern, one local-reading example, and one starter protocol.

CodeTape is not a product. It is a commons blueprint for distributed care.
